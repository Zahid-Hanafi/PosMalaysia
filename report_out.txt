The initiatives  are intended to enhance transparency and convenience for consumers and strengthen the  companys competitiveness in the logistics field.  2.2 History of the Organization  The roots of Pos Malaysia Berhad date back to the early 1800s when postal services  began being provided in the British - colonial Straits Settlements of Penang, Malacca, and  Singapore. These early services provided merely a communication channel for letters,  documents,   and   newspapers,   servicing   both   the   colonial   government   and   the   general  population. After the independence of Malaysia in 1957, the government continued to run  these   services   through   the   Jabatan   Perkhidmatan   Pos   Tanah   Melayu,   which   was   a  government agency running all national postal operations.  One of the key milestones of the organisation's history happened on 1 January 1992  when the Malaysian Postal Services Department became a corporation named Pos Malaysia  Berhad under the Postal Services (Successor Company) Act 1991. Corporatisation meant an  absolute shift from a government department to a commercially oriented organisation, when  all assets and liabilities related to postal services were passed to the new co rporation. The  idea behind the corporatisation was improving the efficiency and performance of the postal  service by applying the business - like approach to its operations.

On 28 August 2001, Pos Malaysia listed on the Kuala Lumpur Stock Exchange via a  reverse takeover of Phileo Allied Berhad, switching itself from a Government - Linked Company  (GLC) to a public listed company. In 2007, Pos Malaysia received its current name Po s  Malaysia Berhad, changing from its previous one of Pos Malaysia and Services Holdings  Berhad. One more restructuring happened in 2011 when DRB - HICOM Berhad acquired  Khazanah Nasional Berhad's 32% equity in the company, thus placing Pos Malaysia in the l ist  of companies belonging to the DRB - HICOM group. In 2016, DRB - HICOM increased their  shareholding in Pos Malaysia.  In 2021, Pos Malaysia entered an important stage of transformation journey, launching  what could be called internally its Phase 1 of transformation named "Back to Black". This stage  focused on solving basic operational and financial problems, such as cost   management and  process optimisation, to stabilise the company financially amidst falling volume of traditional  mail. Phase 2 of the transfo rmation, which was called "Transform the Core" and followed  Phase 1, entailed even deeper technology upgrades and cultural shift towards innovation and  operational excellence. One of the actions taken during this transformation was cloud  migration of Pos M alaysia to Amazon Web Services in 2022, which was done with the help of  Minfy Technologies and AWS Professional Services.  Today, Pos Malaysia Berhad is a national postal and logistics services provider in  Malaysia, having the exclusive mandate under the u niversal service obligation to provide  postal services in the country according to the Postal Services Act 2012. Having a history of  more than 200 years, the company transformed from a simple letter delivering company into  a diversified group of businesses , whose segments cover postal, logistics, aviation, and  others. The company services more than 11 million addresses across Malaysia through a  network of more than 3,500 touchpoints (Pos Malaysia Berhad, 2023).  2 .3 Core Business Activities  There are four major segments of the business of Pos Malaysia Berhad. 3.0 DATA USED IN THE ORGANIZATION  According to the interview done with the representative from Pos Malaysia Berhad, the  organisation has to deal with various types of data in its different business activities. These  data categories play a pivotal role in the day - to - day functioning of the e nterprise's information  systems, especially the SAP ERP system that holds and deals with a vast amount of structured  information for financial reporting, human resource management, and enterprise operations.  The company's   overall ERP database was reported to be around 11 terabytes, a testament to  the data that the system stores continuously.  3.1 Customer Data  Customer data refers to any information about the customers or businesses that utilize  Pos   Malaysia's   services.   This   involves   customer   names,   contact   information,   billing  addresses, service subscription information, account histories, and transaction infor mation.  The data for customers come mainly from the counter transactions carried out in the post  offices, as well as from orders placed online via the company's electronic platforms and  records of parcel deliveries. The CRM system is esp ecially applicable to customer data  management, where it stores interaction data and helps customer service teams handle  customer inquiries and provide good customer service. The SAP ERP system is also used for  customer billing, where the details of the in voices issued and the payment history are stored  for financial purposes.  3.2 Parcel and Logistics Data  Parcel and logistics data is data on the tracking and movement of all mail items and  parcels handled by Pos Malaysia. This includes shipping tracking numbers, parcel size and  weight, shipping routes, shipping times, sender and receiver details, delivery up dates, and  proof of delivery details. In 2023, Pos Malaysia transported over 26 million parcels and over  324 million mail pieces to over 11 million addresses across the country (Pos Malaysia Berhad,  2023). All of this data is   processed using a courier management system that communicates  with barcode scanners, delivery mobile apps and databases of sorting facilities to enable  correct real - time monitoring of parcels throughout the delivery process.

3.3 Financial Data  One of the most comprehensive categories of data within the organisation is financial  data, which is mostly managed by the SAP ERP system. These include accounts receivable  and payable data, billing and invoice data, general ledger data, budget planning da ta, tax data,  payroll data, cost centre allocations, financial reporting outputs etc. The SAP system creates  about 100,000 invoices and handles payroll information for roughly 15,000 employees, based  on the interview. The Finance part o f SAP generates various reports, such as management  reports, EA forms for tax compliance, and payroll slips — all of which are essential for both  internal management and external reporting to regulatory authorities. All the financial data  within the system i s the basis for all statutory financial statement reporting to Bursa Malaysia  and regulatory authorities.  3.4 Employee Data  Employee Data encompasses employees' details of Pos Malaysia Berhad. This entails  personal details, employment agreements, job positions, payroll and benefits information,  attendance and overtime data, training and development records, and performance data . This  type of data is managed by the HR module of the SAP ERP system and is directly connected  with the payroll processing function, enabling accurate salary disbursements to happen on  time. Employee information is sensitive in particul ar because of its statutory deduction details  for EPF, SOCSO and income tax. The HR module in SAP is vast and it caters to all the human  resource activities, which are around 8000 for the HR process, based on the interview. Furthermore, b eyond   these two core alternatives,   it   also recommends four additional  improvements that address the human and day - to - day operational side of the SAP ERP  system. These include improving user training to be specific to each SAP module, increasing  the use of automation in reporting processes, en hancing how the system performs technically,  and making the system easier and more comfortable to use for those who rely on it daily.  Taken together with the two main alternatives, these improvements form a well - rounded plan  that addresses both the technic al and human dimensions of the system.

14.2 Justification of Recommendation  The decision to recommend both alternatives together   comes   from the understanding  that system security and the ability to recover from failures are   both important   to ensure the  effectiveness of the system.   Having strong cybersecurity measures in place is important, but  if the organisation lacks a proper recovery plan, a single serious incident such as a data breach  or server failure could still result in significant data loss and prolonged disruption. On the   other  hand,   having a well - prepared disaster recovery plan means very little if the system remains  poorly protected and vulnerable to threats that could trigger a recovery situation in the first  place.   Furthermore, c onsidering that the SAP ERP system is responsible for processing  payroll for 15,000 employees   every month , handling approximately 100,000 invoices each  cycle, and maintaining financial records that are subject to regulatory reporting requirements  to Bursa Malaysia,   making mistakes in either of these areas ca n have serious consequences.  Especially f or an organisation of Pos Malaysia's size and national responsibility, addressing  both dimensions together is not merely advisable ,   it is necessary.  Furthermore, both alternatives   are comparable   in terms   of   cost,   feasibility,   and  implementation timeline, making it practical for Pos Malaysia to pursue them simultaneously  without placing an unreasonable burden on its IT team or financial resources. The c ombined  implementation of these two initiatives represents the mos t prudent and comprehensive  approach to safeguarding the long - term reliability and integrity of the SAP ERP system.  14.3 Action Plan  The following action plan outlines the key steps and timeline for implementing both  recommended alternatives   simultaneously .  Phase 1   —   Disaster Recovery Planning and Backup Infrastructure (Months 1 – 3)  In the first month, a Business Impact Analysis (BIA) should be conducted to assess the  criticality of each SAP - supported business function and establish formal Recovery Time  Objectives (RTOs) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs). During months two and thre e, a  tiered backup strategy should be   designed and implemented, incorporating full, incremental,  and differential backups stored across both on - premises infrastructure and the AWS cloud  environment. All backup data should be encrypted to protect sensitive HR and financial  records. A formally d ocumented Disaster Recovery Plan covering a range of failure scenarios  should be completed by the end of this phase, with clearly assigned roles, escalation  procedures, and step - by - step restoration sequences.

Phase 2   —   Cybersecurity Enhancement and Performance Monitoring (Months 1 – 3,  concurrent)  Running concurrently with Phase 1, a comprehensive cybersecurity audit of the SAP ERP  system should be initiated in the first month to identify vulnerabilities in access controls, data  encryption,   and   system   configuration.   Based   on   the   audit   findings,   SIEM   tools   and  strengthened role - based access control (RBAC) configurations should be deployed during  months two and three.
